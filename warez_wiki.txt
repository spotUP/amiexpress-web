<p><b>Warez</b> refers to pirated software and other copyrighted digital media—such as video games, movies, music, and e-books—illegally distributed online, often after bypassing <a href="/wiki/Digital_rights_management" title="Digital rights management">digital rights management</a> (DRM). The term, derived from "software", is pronounced like "wares" (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;w&#39; in &#39;wind&#39;">w</span><span title="/ɛ/: &#39;e&#39; in &#39;dress&#39;">ɛ</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;r&#39; in &#39;rye&#39;">r</span><span title="&#39;z&#39; in &#39;zoom&#39;">z</span></span>/</a></span></span>). Warez is typically shared via <a href="/wiki/Peer-to-peer" title="Peer-to-peer">peer-to-peer networks</a>, file-hosting services, and <a href="/wiki/IRC" title="IRC">IRC</a>. The global community involved is known as <b>The Scene</b>. Warez culture dates back to the 1980s and remains embedded in online communities. Its distribution generally violates <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> law and continues to raise legal and ethical concerns.
</p>

<p>Warez, and its <a href="/wiki/Leet" title="Leet">leetspeak</a> form <i>W4r3z</i>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-CollinsDict&#95;1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CollinsDict-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> are plural representations of the word "ware" (short for computer software),<sup id="cite&#95;ref-CollinsDict&#95;1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CollinsDict-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and are terms used to refer to "[p]irated software distributed over the Internet,"<sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> that is, "[s]oftware that has been illegally copied and made available"<sup id="cite&#95;ref-OxfordUSED&#95;4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OxfordUSED-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Chandra2016&#95;5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chandra2016-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> e.g., after having "protection codes de-activated".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-CollinsDict&#95;1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CollinsDict-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> "Cracking", or circumventing copy protection, is an essential part of the warez process,"<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Schwabach1406&#95;6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schwabach1406-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and via this commonality, the definition focused on computer software has been extended to include other forms of material under copyright protection, especially movies.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Schwabach1406&#95;6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schwabach1406-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> As Aaron Schwabach notes, the term covers both supported and unsupported materials (the latter unsupported, termed <a href="/wiki/Abandonware" title="Abandonware">abandonware</a>), and legal recourses aimed at stemming the creation and distribution of warez are designed to cover both profit-driven and "enthusiast" practitioners.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Schwabach1406&#95;6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schwabach1406-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Hence, the term refers to copyrighted works that are distributed without fees or royalties and so traded in general violation of copyright law.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Pirate&#39;s&#95;Forum&#95;7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pirate&#39;s_Forum-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The term <i>warez,</i> which is intended to be pronounced like the word "wares" (/ˈwɛərz/), was coined in the 1990s;<sup id="cite&#95;ref-OxfordUSED&#95;4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OxfordUSED-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> its origin is with an unknown member of an underground computing circle,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> but has since become commonplace among Internet users and the mass media.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> It is used most commonly as a noun:<sup id="cite&#95;ref-OxfordUSED&#95;4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OxfordUSED-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> "My neighbour downloaded 10 gigabytes of warez yesterday"; but has also been used as a verb:<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Pirate&#39;s&#95;Forum&#95;7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pirate&#39;s_Forum-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> "The new Windows was warezed a month before the company officially released it".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Pirate&#39;s&#95;Forum&#95;7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pirate&#39;s_Forum-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The global collection of warez groups has been referred to as "The <a href="/wiki/Warez_scene" title="Warez scene">Warez Scene</a>," or more ambiguously "The Scene."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Witt2015&#95;8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Witt2015-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: p.72">&#58;&#8202;p.72&#8202;</span></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Rehn2004&#95;9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rehn2004-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Chandra2016&#95;5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chandra2016-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>
While the term 'piracy' is commonly used to describe a significant range of activities, most of which are unlawful, the relatively neutral meaning in this context is "...mak[ing] use of or reproduc[ing] the work of another without authorization".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Some groups (including the GNU project of the <a href="/wiki/Free_Software_Foundation" title="Free Software Foundation">Free Software Foundation</a>, FSF) object to the use of this and other words such as "theft" because they represent an attempt to create a particular impression in the reader:<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1244412712">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 32px}.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;margin-top:0}@media(min-width:500px){.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{padding-left:1.6em}}</style></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Publishers often refer to prohibited copying as "piracy." In this way, they imply that illegal copying is ethically equivalent to attacking ships on the high seas, kidnapping and murdering the people on them.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-w2a&#95;11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-w2a-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> </p></blockquote><p>The FSF advocates the use of terms like "prohibited copying" or "unauthorized copying", or "sharing information with your neighbor."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-w2a&#95;11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-w2a-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Hence, the term "software pirate" is controversial; FSF derides its use, while many self-described pirates take pride in the term,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and some groups (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Pirates_with_Attitudes" title="Pirates with Attitudes">Pirates with Attitudes</a>) fully embrace it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p><i>Direct download [DDL] sites</i> are web locations that index links to locations where files can be directly downloaded to the user's computer; many such sites link to free <a href="/wiki/File_hosting_service" class="mw-redirect" title="File hosting service">file hosting services</a>, for the hosting of materials.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> DDL sites do not directly host the material and can avoid the fees that normally accompany large file hosting.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Motivations_and_arguments">Motivations and arguments</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Motivations and arguments"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>

<p>The production and/or distribution of warez is illegal in most countries due to the protections provided in the <a href="/wiki/TRIPS_Agreement" title="TRIPS Agreement">TRIPS Agreement</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Software <a href="/wiki/Copyright_infringement" title="Copyright infringement">infringers</a> generally exploit the international nature of the <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> issue to avoid law enforcement in specific countries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Violations are typically overlooked in poorer <a href="/wiki/Third_world" class="mw-redirect" title="Third world">third world</a> countries, and other countries with weak or non-existent protection for intellectual property.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Additionally, some first world countries have loopholes in legislation that allow the warez to continue.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>There is also a movement, exemplified by groups like <a href="/wiki/The_Pirate_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="The Pirate Party">The Pirate Party</a> and scholars at <a href="/wiki/The_Mises_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="The Mises Institute">The Mises Institute</a>, that the very idea of <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_property" title="Intellectual property">intellectual property</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Anathema" title="Anathema">anathema</a> to free society.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This is in contrast to some of the more traditional <a href="/wiki/Open_source" title="Open source">open source</a> advocates such as <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Lessig" title="Lawrence Lessig">Lawrence Lessig</a>, who advocate for middle ground between freedom and intellectual property.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Legality">Legality</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legality"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>

<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1236090951" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Copyright_infringement#Legality" title="Copyright infringement">Copyright infringement §&#160;Legality</a></div>
<p>Generally, there are four elements of criminal copyright infringement: the existence of a valid copyright, that copyright was infringed, the infringement was willful, and the infringement was either substantial, or for commercial gain (at levels often set by <a href="/wiki/Statute" title="Statute">statute</a>).<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Goldman2004&#95;21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldman2004-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Offering warez is generally understood to be a form of <a href="/wiki/Copyright_infringement" title="Copyright infringement">copyright infringement</a> that is punishable as either a <a href="/wiki/Tort" title="Tort">civil wrong</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Criminal_law" title="Criminal law">crime</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Goldman2004&#95;21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldman2004-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 6">&#58;&#8202;6&#8202;</span></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Schwabach1406&#95;6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schwabach1406-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page: 307">&#58;&#8202;307&#8202;</span></sup>
</p><p>Often, sites hosting torrent files claim that they are not breaking any laws because they are not offering the actual data; rather, the sites only offer a link to other places or peers that contain the infringing material.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> However, many prosecution cases and convictions argue to the contrary. For instance, Dimitri Mader, the French national who operates a movie distribution warez site, Wawa-Mania, was fined 20,000 € and sentenced, in absentia, to a year in jail by a European court (after fleeing France for the Philippines), for his role in managing the site.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-AndyTF15&#95;24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AndyTF15-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In the U.S., through 2004, more than 80 individuals had been prosecuted and convicted for trade in warez products (under the <a href="/wiki/NET_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="NET Act">NET Act</a> and other statutes), for movie and software pirating in particular, with a number of individuals being imprisoned, including some enthusiast traders.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Schwabach1406&#95;6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schwabach1406-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: p.308">&#58;&#8202;p.308&#8202;</span></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>However, laws and their application to warez activities may vary greatly from country to country;<sup id="cite&#95;ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> for instance, while Wawa-Mania is under sanction in France, it remains in operation via a host in Moldova, and through use of an Ecuadorian top-level domain.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-AndyTF15&#95;24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AndyTF15-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Hence, while high-profile web hosts and domain providers<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (August 2016)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> generally do not permit the hosting of warez, and delete sites found to be hosting them,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> private endeavours and small commercial entities continue to allow the trade in warez to continue.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Distribution">Distribution</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1236090951" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Warez_scene" title="Warez scene">Warez scene</a></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Warez_Hierarchy.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Warez_Hierarchy.svg/250px-Warez_Hierarchy.svg.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="487" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Warez_Hierarchy.svg/375px-Warez_Hierarchy.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Warez_Hierarchy.svg/500px-Warez_Hierarchy.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="213" data-file-height="415" /></a><figcaption>Warez scene hierarchy.</figcaption></figure>
<p>Warez are often distributed outside of <a href="/wiki/Warez_scene" title="Warez scene">The Scene</a> (a collection of warez groups) by <a href="/wiki/.torrent" class="mw-redirect" title=".torrent">torrents</a> (files including tracker info, piece size, uncompressed file size, comments, and vary in size from 1&#160;k, to 400&#160;k.) uploaded to a popular P2P website by an associate or friend of the cracker or cracking crew. An <a href="/wiki/.nfo" title=".nfo">nfo</a> or <a href="/wiki/FILE_ID.DIZ" title="FILE ID.DIZ">FILE ID.DIZ</a> is often made to promote who created the release. It is then leeched (downloaded) by users of the <a href="/wiki/BitTorrent_tracker" title="BitTorrent tracker">tracker</a> and spread to other sharing sites using P2P, or other sources such as <a href="/wiki/Newsgroup#Binary_newsgroups" class="mw-redirect" title="Newsgroup">newsgroups</a>. From there, it can be downloaded by millions of users all over the world. Often, one release is duplicated, renamed, then re-uploaded to different sites so that eventually, it can become impossible to trace the original file. Another increasingly popular method of distributing Warez is via <a href="/wiki/One-click_hosting" class="mw-redirect" title="One-click hosting">one-click hosting</a> websites.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In the early 1990s, warez were often published on bulletin boards that had a warez section.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Rise_of_software_infringement">Rise of software infringement</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Rise of software infringement"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1305433154" /><table class="box-Unreferenced&#95;section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><span typeof="mw:File"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/60px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/120px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></span></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a href="/wiki/Special:EditPage/Warez" title="Special:EditPage/Warez">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Help:Referencing for beginners">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span>  <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">March 2009</span>)</i></span><span class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<small><a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this message</a></small>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Unauthorized copying has been an ongoing phenomenon that started when high quality, commercially produced software was released for sale. Whether the medium was cassette tape or floppy disk, cracking enthusiasts found a way to duplicate the software and spread it without the permission of the maker. Bootlegging communities were built around the <a href="/wiki/Apple_II" title="Apple II">Apple II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Commodore_64" title="Commodore 64">Commodore 64</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atari_8-bit_computers" title="Atari 8-bit computers">Atari 8-bit computers</a>, <a href="/wiki/ZX_Spectrum" title="ZX Spectrum">ZX Spectrum</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Amiga" title="Amiga">Amiga</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Atari_ST" title="Atari ST">Atari ST</a> and other systems. Entire networks of <a href="/wiki/Bulletin_board_system" title="Bulletin board system">BBSes</a> sprang up to traffic illegal software from one user to the next. Machines like the <a href="/wiki/Amiga" title="Amiga">Amiga</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Commodore_64" title="Commodore 64">Commodore 64</a> had an international network, through which software not available on one continent would eventually make its way to every region via <a href="/wiki/Bulletin_board_system" title="Bulletin board system">bulletin board systems</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>It was also common in the 1980s to use physical floppy disks and the <a href="/wiki/Mail" title="Mail">postal service</a> for spreading software, in an activity known as <i>mail trading</i>. Prior to the sale of software that came on CD-ROM discs and after hard drives had become available, the software did not require the floppy disc to be in the drive when starting and using the program. So, a user could install it onto their computer and mail the disk to the next person, who could do the same. Particularly widespread in continental Europe, mail trading was even used by many of the leading <a href="/wiki/Software_cracking" title="Software cracking">cracker</a> groups as their primary channel of interaction. Software copyright violation via mail trading was also the most common means for many computer hobbyists in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_bloc" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern bloc">Eastern bloc</a> countries to receive new Western software for their computers.
</p>
<figure class="mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Pftp-99.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Pftp-99.png/250px-Pftp-99.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="150" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Pftp-99.png/500px-Pftp-99.png 1.5x" data-file-width="1964" data-file-height="1181" /></a><figcaption>A screen shot of <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886049734">.mw-parser-output .monospaced{font-family:monospace,monospace}</style><span class="monospaced">pftp</span> logged into several FTP sites. This tool was used by couriers to quickly and easily move large amounts of software from one site to many others. Since couriering was competitive, using this tool was considered cheating.</figcaption></figure>
<p>Copy-protection schemes for the early systems were designed to defeat casual duplication attempts, as "crackers" would typically release a copied game to the "pirate" community the day they were earmarked for market.
</p><p>A famous event in the history of software copyright policy was an <a href="/wiki/Open_Letter_to_Hobbyists" class="mw-redirect" title="Open Letter to Hobbyists">open letter</a> written by <a href="/wiki/Bill_Gates" title="Bill Gates">Bill Gates</a> of <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>, dated February 3, 1976, in which he argued that the quality of available software would increase if "software piracy" were less prevalent. However, until the early 1990s, software copyright infringement was not yet considered a serious problem by most people. In 1992, the <a href="/wiki/Software_Publishers_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="Software Publishers Association">Software Publishers Association</a> began to battle against this phenomenon, with its promotional video "<a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Copy_That_Floppy" title="Don&#39;t Copy That Floppy">Don't Copy That Floppy</a>". It and the <a href="/wiki/Business_Software_Alliance" class="mw-redirect" title="Business Software Alliance">Business Software Alliance</a> have remained the most active anti-infringement organizations worldwide, although to compensate for extensive growth in recent years, they have gained the assistance of the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA), the <a href="/wiki/Motion_Picture_Association_of_America" class="mw-redirect" title="Motion Picture Association of America">Motion Picture Association of America</a> (MPAA), as well as <a href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Composers,_Authors,_and_Publishers" class="mw-redirect" title="American Society of Composers, Authors, and Publishers">American Society of Composers, Authors, and Publishers</a> (ASCAP) and <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_Music_Incorporated" class="mw-redirect" title="Broadcast Music Incorporated">Broadcast Music Incorporated</a> (BMI).
</p><p>Today most warez files are distributed to the public via <a href="/wiki/BitTorrent_(protocol)" class="mw-redirect" title="BitTorrent (protocol)">bittorrent</a> and <a href="/wiki/One-click_hosting" class="mw-redirect" title="One-click hosting">One-click hosting sites</a>. Some of the most popular software companies that are being targeted are Adobe, Microsoft, Nero, Apple, DreamWorks, and Autodesk, to name a few. To reduce the spread of illegal copying, some companies have hired people to release "fake" torrents (known as <a href="/wiki/Torrent_poisoning" title="Torrent poisoning">Torrent poisoning</a>), which look real and are meant to be downloaded, but while downloading the individual does not realize that the company that owns the software has received their <a href="/wiki/IP_address" title="IP address">IP address</a>. They will then contact their <a href="/wiki/Internet_service_provider" title="Internet service provider">ISP</a>, and further legal action may be taken by the company/ISP.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Causes_that_have_accelerated_its_growth">Causes that have accelerated its growth</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Causes that have accelerated its growth"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1305433154" /><table class="box-Unreferenced&#95;section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><span typeof="mw:File"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/60px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/120px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></span></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a href="/wiki/Special:EditPage/Warez" title="Special:EditPage/Warez">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Help:Referencing for beginners">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span>  <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">August 2016</span>)</i></span><span class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<small><a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this message</a></small>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Similar to <a href="/wiki/Televisions" class="mw-redirect" title="Televisions">televisions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Telephones" class="mw-redirect" title="Telephones">telephones</a>, computers have become a necessity to every person in the <a href="/wiki/Information_age" class="mw-redirect" title="Information age">information age</a>. As the use of computers increased, so had <a href="/wiki/Cybercrime" title="Cybercrime">software and cyber crimes</a>.
</p><p>In the mid-1990s, the average <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> user was still on <a href="/wiki/Dial-up" class="mw-redirect" title="Dial-up">dial-up</a>, with average speed ranging between 28.8 and 33.6 <a href="/wiki/Kilobit_per_second" class="mw-redirect" title="Kilobit per second">kbit/s</a>. If one wished to download a piece of software, which could run about 200 <a href="/wiki/Megabyte" title="Megabyte">MB</a>, the download time could be longer than one day, depending on network traffic, the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Service_Provider" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet Service Provider">Internet Service Provider</a>, and the server. Around 1997, <a href="/wiki/Broadband" title="Broadband">broadband</a> began to gain popularity due to its greatly increased network speeds. As "large-sized file transfer" problems became less severe, warez became more widespread and began to affect large <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">software</a> files like <a href="/wiki/Animations" class="mw-redirect" title="Animations">animations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">movies</a>.
</p><p>In the past, files were distributed by <a href="/wiki/Point-to-point_(telecommunications)" title="Point-to-point (telecommunications)">point-to-point</a> technology: with a central uploader distributing files to downloaders. With these systems, a large number of downloaders for a popular file uses an increasingly larger amount of <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(computing)" title="Bandwidth (computing)">bandwidth</a>. If there are too many downloads, the server can become unavailable. The opposite is true for <a href="/wiki/Peer-to-peer" title="Peer-to-peer">peer-to-peer</a> networking; the <i>more</i> downloaders the <i>faster</i> the file distribution is. With swarming technology as implemented in <a href="/wiki/File_sharing" title="File sharing">file sharing</a> systems like <a href="/wiki/EDonkey2000" title="EDonkey2000">eDonkey2000</a> or <a href="/wiki/BitTorrent_(protocol)" class="mw-redirect" title="BitTorrent (protocol)">BitTorrent</a>, downloaders help the uploader by picking up some of its uploading responsibilities. There are many sites with links to One-click hosting websites and other sites where one can upload files that contribute to the growing amount of warez.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Distribution_via_compromised_FTP_servers">Distribution via compromised FTP servers</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Distribution via compromised FTP servers"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Prior to the development of modern peer-to-peer sharing systems and home broadband service, sharing warez sometimes involved warez groups scanning the Internet for weakly secured computer systems with high-speed connections. These weakly secured systems would be compromised by exploiting the poor <a href="/wiki/File_Transfer_Protocol" title="File Transfer Protocol">FTP</a> security, creating a special directory on the server with an unassuming name to contain the illegal content.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Koziol&#95;2003&#95;72&#95;30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Koziol_2003_72-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>A common mistake of early FTP administrators was to permit a directory named /incoming that allows full read and write access by external users, but the files themselves in /incoming were hidden; by creating a directory inside /incoming, this hidden directory would then allow normal file viewing.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Users of the compromised site would be directed to log in and go to a location such as /incoming/data/warez to find the warez content. Messages could be left for other warez users by uploading a plain text file with the message inside.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Hackers would also use known software bugs to illicitly gain full administrative remote control over a computer, and install a hidden FTP service to host their wares.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This FTP service was usually running on an unusual port number, or with a non-anonymous login name like "login: warez / Password: warez" to help prevent discovery by legitimate users; information about this compromised system would then be distributed to a select group of people who were part of the warez scene.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>It was important for warez group members to regulate who had access to these compromised FTP servers, to keep the network bandwidth usage low.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> A site that suddenly became very popular would be noticed by the real owners of the equipment, as their business systems became slow or low on disk space; investigation of system usage would then inevitably result in discovery and removal of the warez, and tightening of the site security.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Automated_warez_distribution_via_IRC_bots">Automated warez distribution via IRC bots</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Automated warez distribution via IRC bots"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>As the ability to compromise and attain full remote control of business servers became more developed, the warez groups would hack a server and install an <a href="/wiki/IRC" title="IRC">IRC</a> bot on the compromised systems alongside the <a href="/wiki/File_Transfer_Protocol" title="File Transfer Protocol">FTP</a> service, or the IRC bot would provide file sharing directly by itself. This software would intelligently regulate access to the illicit data by using file queues to limit bandwidth usage, or by only running during off-hours overnight when the business owning the compromised hardware was closed for the day.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Koziol&#95;2003&#95;72&#95;30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Koziol_2003_72-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In order to advertise the existence of the compromised site, the IRC software would join public IRC <i>warez</i> channels as a <i>bot</i> and post into the channel with occasional status messages every few minutes, providing information about how many people are logged into the warez host, how many files are currently being downloaded, what the upload/download ratio is (to force users into contributing data of their own before they can download), which warez distributor is running the bot, and other status information.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>This functionality still exists and can still be found on IRC <i>warez</i> channels, as an alternative to the modern and streamlined <a href="/wiki/Peer-to-peer" title="Peer-to-peer">P2P</a> distribution systems.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The opportunity to find and compromise poorly secured systems on which to create an illicit warez distribution site has only increased with the popular use of broadband service by home users who may not fully understand the security implications of having their home computer always turned on and connected to the Internet<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<p>There is generally a distinction made between different sub-types of warez. The unusual spellings shown here were commonly used as directory names within a compromised server, to organize the files rather than having them all thrown together in a single random collection.
</p>
<ul><li><b>Apps</b> – <a href="/wiki/Application_software" title="Application software">Applications</a>: Generally a retail version of a software package.</li>
<li><b>Cracks</b> – Cracked applications: A modified executable or more (usually one) and/or a library (usually one) or more and/or a patch designed to turn a trial version of a software package into the full version and/or bypass <a href="/wiki/Digital_rights_management" title="Digital rights management">copy protections</a>.</li>
<li><b>Dox</b> – <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">Video game</a> add-ons: These include NoCDs, cracks, trainers, cheat codes etc.</li>
<li><b>EBook</b> – <a href="/wiki/E-book" class="mw-redirect" title="E-book">E-books</a>: These include unlicensed copies of e-books, scanned books, scanned comics, etc.</li>
<li><b>Games</b> – Video games: This scene concentrates on both computer and console games, often released as <a href="/wiki/ISO_image" class="mw-redirect" title="ISO image">ISO</a> or other format disk image.</li>
<li><b>Hacks</b> – <a href="/wiki/Security_hacker" title="Security hacker">Simplified/Specific Hacking Tools</a>: Programs designed to perform specific hacks at the click of a button, typically with a limited scope, such as <a href="/wiki/AOHell" title="AOHell">AOHell</a> and AOL4Free.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>Keygens</b> – <a href="/wiki/Keygen" title="Keygen">Keygen</a> software are tools that replicate the registration/activation process of a genuine software product and generate the necessary <a href="/wiki/Product_key" title="Product key">keys</a> to activate the software.</li>
<li><b>Movies</b> – <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">Movies</a>: Unauthorized copies of movies, can be released while still in theaters or from CDs/DVDs/Blu-ray prior to the actual retail date.</li>
<li><b>MP3s</b> – <a href="/wiki/MP3" title="MP3">MP3</a> audio: Audio from albums, singles, or other sources usually obtained by <a href="/wiki/Ripping" title="Ripping">ripping a CD</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Radio_music_ripping" title="Radio music ripping">radio broadcast</a> and released in the compressed audio format MP3.</li>
<li><b>MVs/MVids</b> – <a href="/wiki/Music_videos" class="mw-redirect" title="Music videos">Music videos</a> – Can be ripped from TV, HDTV, DVDs or VCDs.</li>
<li><b>NoCD</b>, <b>NoDVD</b>, <b>FixedExe</b> – A file modification that allows an installed program to be run without inserting the CD or DVD into the drive.</li>
<li><b>RIP</b> – A game that <a href="/wiki/Portable_application" title="Portable application">doesn't have to be installed</a>; any required registry entry can be included as a .reg file. RIP games can be ripped of music and/or video files, or, for video games, <a href="/wiki/ROM_image" title="ROM image">ROMs</a>, thus decreasing the size of the download.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> RIPs with nothing ripped out sometimes are referred to as DP (direct play).</li>
<li><b>Portables</b> – <a href="/wiki/Portable_applications" class="mw-redirect" title="Portable applications">Portable applications</a>: Similar to RIPs, but in this case they're software applications instead of video games. The point of portable software is the fact that it can be placed on removable media (or any place on the local hard drive) and doesn't need installing; usually it is compressed into one executable file, by using software like <a href="/wiki/VMware_ThinApp" title="VMware ThinApp">VMware ThinApp</a> or <a href="/wiki/MoleBox" class="mw-redirect" title="MoleBox">MoleBox</a>.</li>
<li><b>Scripts</b> – <a href="/wiki/Scripting_language" title="Scripting language">Scripts</a>: These include unlicensed copies of commercial scripts (such as vBulletin, Invision Power Board, etc.) coded by companies in <a href="/wiki/PHP" title="PHP">PHP</a>, <a href="/wiki/Active_Server_Pages" title="Active Server Pages">ASP</a>, and other languages.</li>
<li><b>Subs</b> – <a href="/wiki/Subtitles" title="Subtitles">Subtitles</a>: can be integrated in a TV-Rip or Movie.</li>
<li><b>Serials</b> – Refers to a collection of <a href="/wiki/Product_key" title="Product key">product keys</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Serial_numbers" class="mw-redirect" title="Serial numbers">serial numbers</a> made available for the purpose of activating trial software, without payment.</li>
<li><b>Templates</b> – <a href="/wiki/Web_template" class="mw-redirect" title="Web template">Web templates</a>: These include leaked commercial website templates coded by companies.</li>
<li><b>TV-Rips</b> – <a href="/wiki/TV" class="mw-redirect" title="TV">Television programs</a>: Television shows or movies, usually with commercials edited out. Commonly released within a few hours after airing. DVD Rips of television series fall under this sub-type.</li>
<li><b>XXX</b> – <a href="/wiki/Pornography" title="Pornography">Pornography</a>: These can be imagesets, <a href="/wiki/Paysite" title="Paysite">paysite</a> videos or retail movies.</li>
<li><b>Zero-day</b> or <b>0-day</b> – Any copyrighted work that has been released the same day as the original product, or sometimes even before.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-gehring&#95;38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gehring-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It is considered a mark of skill among warez distribution groups to crack and distribute a program on the same day as its commercial release.</li></ul>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Movie_infringement">Movie infringement</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Movie infringement"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>

<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1236090951" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pirated_movie_release_types" title="Pirated movie release types">Pirated movie release types</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">Movie</a> <a href="/wiki/Copyright_infringement" title="Copyright infringement">copyright infringement</a> was looked upon as impossible by the major studios. When <a href="/wiki/Dial-up" class="mw-redirect" title="Dial-up">dial-up</a> was common in early and mid-1990s, movies distributed on the Internet tended to be small. The technique that was usually used to make them small was to use <a href="/wiki/Video_compression" class="mw-redirect" title="Video compression">compression</a> software, thus lowering the video quality significantly. At that time, the largest copyright violation threat was software.
</p><p>However, along with the rise in <a href="/wiki/Broadband" title="Broadband">broadband</a> internet connections beginning around 1998, higher quality movies began to see widespread distribution – with the release of <a href="/wiki/DeCSS" title="DeCSS">DeCSS</a>, <a href="/wiki/ISO_9660" title="ISO 9660">ISO</a> images copied directly from the original DVDs were slowly becoming a feasible distribution method. Today, movie sharing has become so common that it has caused major concern amongst movie studios and their representative organizations. Because of this the <a href="/wiki/MPAA" class="mw-redirect" title="MPAA">MPAA</a> is often running campaigns during movie trailers where it tries to discourage people from copying material without permission. Unlike the music industry, which has had online music stores available for several years, the movie industry moved to online distribution only in 2006, after the launch of <a href="/wiki/Amazon_Unbox" class="mw-redirect" title="Amazon Unbox">Amazon Unbox</a>.
</p><p>Because of this, cameras are sometimes forbidden in movie theaters.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="File_formats">File formats</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: File formats"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1305433154" /><table class="box-Unreferenced&#95;section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><span typeof="mw:File"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/60px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/120px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></span></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a href="/wiki/Special:EditPage/Warez" title="Special:EditPage/Warez">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Help:Referencing for beginners">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1236090951" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Standard_(warez)" class="mw-redirect" title="Standard (warez)">Standard (warez)</a></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc" title="Compact disc">CD</a> software release can contain up to 700&#160;<a href="/wiki/Megabytes" class="mw-redirect" title="Megabytes">megabytes</a> of data, which presented challenges when sending over the Internet, particularly in the late 1990s when broadband was unavailable to most home consumers. These challenges apply to an even greater extent for a single-layer <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> release, which can contain up to 4.7&#160;<a href="/wiki/Gigabyte" title="Gigabyte">GB</a> of data. The warez scene made it standard practice to split releases up into many separate pieces, called disks, using several file compression formats: (historical <a href="/wiki/Tar_(file_format)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tar (file format)">TAR</a>, <a href="/wiki/LHA_(file_format)" title="LHA (file format)">LZH</a>, <a href="/wiki/ACE_(compression_file_format)" class="mw-redirect" title="ACE (compression file format)">ACE</a>, UHA, <a href="/wiki/ARJ" title="ARJ">ARJ</a>), <a href="/wiki/ZIP_(file_format)" title="ZIP (file format)">ZIP</a>, and most commonly <a href="/wiki/RAR_(file_format)" title="RAR (file format)">RAR</a>. The original purpose of these "disks" was so that each .rar file could fit on a single 1.44&#160;MB 3½ inch <a href="/wiki/Floppy_disk" title="Floppy disk">floppy disk</a>. With the growing size of games, this is no longer feasible, as hundreds of disks would need to be used. The average size of disks released by groups today are 50&#160;<a href="/wiki/Megabytes" class="mw-redirect" title="Megabytes">megabytes</a> or 100&#160;megabytes, however it is common to find disks up to 200&#160;megabytes.
</p><p>This method has many advantages over sending a single large file:
</p>
<ul><li>The two-layer compression could sometimes achieve almost a tenfold improvement over the original DVD/CD image. The overall file size is cut down and lessens the transfer time and bandwidth required.</li>
<li>If there is a problem during the file transfer and data was corrupted, it is only necessary to resend the few corrupted RAR files instead of resending the entire large file.</li>
<li>This method also allows the possibility of downloading separate ‘disks’ from different sources, as an early attempt at modern segmented downloading.
<ul><li>In the case of One-click hosting websites, downloading multiple files from one or several sources can significantly increase download speeds. This is because even if the source(s) provides slow download speeds on individual disks, downloading several disks simultaneously will allow the user to achieve much greater download rates.</li></ul></li></ul>
<p>Despite the fact that many modern ftp programs support segmented downloading, the compression via RAR, ZIP, and breaking up of files has not changed.
</p><p>Releases of software titles often come in two forms. The <b>full</b> form is a full version of a game or application, generally released as CD or DVD-writable <a href="/wiki/Disk_images" class="mw-redirect" title="Disk images">disk images</a> (<a href="/wiki/ISO_image" class="mw-redirect" title="ISO image">BIN</a> or <a href="/wiki/ISO_9660_image" class="mw-redirect" title="ISO 9660 image">ISO</a> files). A <b>rip</b> is a cut-down version of the title in which additions included on the legitimate DVD/CD (generally <a href="/wiki/Portable_Document_Format" class="mw-redirect" title="Portable Document Format">PDF</a> manuals, help files, tutorials, and audio/video media) are omitted. In a game rip, generally all game video is removed, and the audio is compressed to MP3 or <a href="/wiki/Vorbis" title="Vorbis">Vorbis</a>, which must then be decoded to its original form before playing. These rips are very rare today, as most modern <a href="/wiki/Broadband" title="Broadband">broadband</a> connections can easily handle the full files, and the audio is usually already compressed by the original producer in some fashion.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Warez_and_malware">Warez and malware</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Warez and malware"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>There is a common perception that warez sites represent high risk in terms of <a href="/wiki/Malware" title="Malware">malware</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-mobilemalware&#95;39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mobilemalware-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In addition, there are several papers showing there is indeed correlation between warez/file sharing sites and <a href="/wiki/Malware" title="Malware">malware</a>. In particular, one study<sup id="cite&#95;ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> shows that out of all domains the study classified as <i>pirate</i>, 7.1% are infected (while out of <i>random</i> domains only 0.4% were infected); another study<sup id="cite&#95;ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> maintains that '"maliciousness" of the content for sites they classified as <i>pirate</i> (which specifically included <i>warez</i> sites) is the highest among all the researched site categories. Domains related to anti-copy protection tools are among the most malicious sites.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Kammerstetter:2012:VCM:2382196.2382282&#95;42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kammerstetter:2012:VCM:2382196.2382282-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Ikinci08monkey-spider:detecting&#95;43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ikinci08monkey-spider:detecting-43"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Another study specifically targeted anti-copy protection tools such as cracks and key generators. They conclude that the majority of these programs aim to infect the user's computer with one or more types of malware. The chance of the end-user being exposed to malicious code when dealing with cracked applications or games is more than 50%.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Kammerstetter:2012:VCM:2382196.2382282&#95;42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kammerstetter:2012:VCM:2382196.2382282-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Infected warez directly from the <a href="/wiki/Warez_scene" title="Warez scene">warez scene</a> on the other hand, is a very unusual occurrence. The malicious content is usually added at a later stage by third parties.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>44<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Demographics">Demographics</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Demographics"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Warez traders share many similarities to both hackers and crackers. Primary warez distribution groups include a single cracker that renders all copy protected technologies in a warez inoperable. Unlike the typical age of a hacker and cracker, the general age and demographics of a warez trader are older and married IT professionals. The need for attention is another similarity between the groups as well as the belief that digital property should be free.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>45<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>46<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
