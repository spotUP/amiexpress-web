					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Device for hacking telephone networks</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1236090951">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about the <a href="/wiki/Phone_phreaking" class="mw-redirect" title="Phone phreaking">phone phreaking</a> tool. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Blue_box_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Blue box (disambiguation)">Blue box (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<figure typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Blue_Box_at_the_Powerhouse_Museum.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Blue_Box_at_the_Powerhouse_Museum.jpg/330px-Blue_Box_at_the_Powerhouse_Museum.jpg" decoding="async" width="280" height="173" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Blue_Box_at_the_Powerhouse_Museum.jpg/500px-Blue_Box_at_the_Powerhouse_Museum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Blue_Box_at_the_Powerhouse_Museum.jpg/960px-Blue_Box_at_the_Powerhouse_Museum.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4236" data-file-height="2621" /></a><figcaption>Blue box designed and built by <a href="/wiki/Steve_Wozniak" title="Steve Wozniak">Steve Wozniak</a> and sold by <a href="/wiki/Steve_Jobs" title="Steve Jobs">Steve Jobs</a> before they founded <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple</a>. Displayed at the <a href="/wiki/Powerhouse_Museum" title="Powerhouse Museum">Powerhouse Museum</a>, from the collection of the <a href="/wiki/Computer_History_Museum" title="Computer History Museum">Computer History Museum</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></figcaption></figure>
<p>A <b>blue box</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronic device</a> that produces tones used to generate the <a href="/wiki/In-band_signaling" title="In-band signaling">in-band signaling</a> tones formerly used within the North American long-distance telephone network to send line status and called number information over voice circuits. During that period, charges associated with <a href="/wiki/Long-distance_calling" title="Long-distance calling">long-distance calling</a> were commonplace and could be significant, depending on the time, duration and destination of the call. A blue box device allowed for circumventing these charges by enabling an illicit user, referred to as a "<a href="/wiki/Phreaking" title="Phreaking">phreaker</a>", to place long-distance calls, without using the network's user facilities, that would be billed to another number or dismissed entirely by the telecom company's billing system as an incomplete call. A number of similar "color boxes" were also created to control other aspects of the phone network.
</p><p>First developed in the 1960s and used by a small phreaker community, the introduction of low-cost microelectronics in the early 1970s greatly simplified these devices to the point where they could be constructed by anyone reasonably competent with a <a href="/wiki/Soldering_iron" title="Soldering iron">soldering iron</a> or <a href="/wiki/Breadboard" title="Breadboard">breadboard</a> construction. Soon after, models of relatively low quality were being offered fully assembled, but these often required tinkering by the user to remain operational.
</p><p>Over time, as the long-distance network became digitized, the audio call-control tones were replaced with <a href="/wiki/Out-of-band_signaling" class="mw-redirect" title="Out-of-band signaling">out-of-band signaling</a> methods in the form of <a href="/wiki/Common-channel_signaling" title="Common-channel signaling">common-channel signaling</a> (CCS) carried digitally on a separate channel inaccessible to the telephone user. This development limited the usefulness of audio-tone-based blue boxes by the 1980s, and they are of little to no use today.
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Automated_dialing">Automated dialing</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_box&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Automated dialing"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Local calling had been increasingly automated through the first half of the 20th century, but long-distance calling still required operator intervention. Automation was deemed essential by <a href="/wiki/AT%26T_Corporation" title="AT&amp;T Corporation">AT&amp;T</a>. By the 1940s they had developed a system that used audible tones played over the long-distance lines to control network connections. Tone pairs, referred to as multi-frequency (MF) signals, were assigned to the digits used for telephone numbers. A different, single tone, referred to as single frequency (SF), was used as a line status signal.
</p><p>This new system allowed the telephone network to be increasingly automated by deploying the dialers and tone generators on an as-required basis, starting with the busier exchanges. <a href="/wiki/Bell_Labs" title="Bell Labs">Bell Labs</a> was happy to advertise their success in creating this system, and repeatedly revealed details of its inner workings. In the February 1950 issue of <i><a href="/wiki/Popular_Electronics" title="Popular Electronics">Popular Electronics</a></i>, they published an advertisement, <i>Playing a Tune for a Telephone Number</i>, which showed the musical notes for the digits on a staff and described the telephone operator's pushbuttons as a "musical keyboard".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Two keys on a piano would need to be pushed simultaneously to play the tones for each digit. The illustration did not include the tone pairs for the special control signals KP and ST, although in the picture the operator's finger on the KP key and the ST key is visible. In the 1950s, AT&amp;T released a public relations film, "Speeding Speech", which described the operation of the system. In the film, the tone sequence for sending a complete telephone number is heard through a loudspeaker as a technician presses the keys for dialing.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In November 1954, the <a href="/wiki/Bell_System_Technical_Journal" class="mw-redirect" title="Bell System Technical Journal">Bell System Technical Journal</a> published an article entitled "In-Band Single-Frequency Signaling", which described the signaling scheme used for starting and ending telephone calls for the purpose of routing over <a href="/wiki/Trunking" title="Trunking">trunk lines</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In November 1960, an article in the Bell System Technical Journal provided an overview of the technical details of signaling systems, and disclosed the frequencies of the signals.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The system was relatively complex for 1950s technology. It had to accurately decode the frequencies and ignore any signals where that frequency might be accidentally created; music playing in the background might randomly contain the SF tones and the system had to filter these out. To do this, the signaling unit compared the signal power from a bandpass filter centered on 2600<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz to signal power in other parts of the audio band, and only triggered if the tone was the most prominent signal. The originating end of the call would play the tone into the trunk line when the call ended, and trigger the remote end to end the call. After a short time, the originating end reduced the tone level and continued to send tone as long as it received on hook status from its local equipment.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Discovery_and_early_use">Discovery and early use</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_box&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Discovery and early use"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Before the technical details were published, many users discovered unintentionally, and to their annoyance, that a 2600 <a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">Hz</a> tone played into the caller's handset would cause a <a href="/wiki/Long-distance_calling" title="Long-distance calling">long-distance call</a> to disconnect. The 2600<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz tone might be present if the caller were whistling into the telephone microphone while waiting for the called party to answer. Upon detecting the tone from the caller's end, the receiving signaling unit sent an on hook status to the connected equipment, which disconnected the call from that point forward, as if the caller had hung up.
</p><p>Among the earliest to discover this effect was <a href="/wiki/Joybubbles" title="Joybubbles">Joe Engressia</a>, known as <i>Joybubbles</i>, who accidentally discovered it at the age of seven by <a href="/wiki/Whistling" title="Whistling">whistling</a>. He became fascinated with the phone network, and over the next decade had built up a considerable base of knowledge about the system and how to place calls using the control tones.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> He and other <a href="/wiki/Phone_phreak" class="mw-redirect" title="Phone phreak">phone phreaks</a>, such as "<a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_from_New_York&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bill from New York (page does not exist)">Bill from New York</a>" and "The Glitch", trained themselves to whistle 2600<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz to reset a trunk line. They also learned how to route telephone calls by flashing, which is using very short pulses of the on-hook signal to send routing instructions.
</p><p>At one point in the 1960s, packages of the <a href="/wiki/Cap%27n_Crunch" title="Cap&#39;n Crunch">Cap'n Crunch</a> breakfast cereal included a free gift: a small whistle that, by coincidence, generated a 2600<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz tone when one of the whistle's two holes was covered.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The phreaker <a href="/wiki/John_Draper" title="John Draper">John Draper</a> adopted his nickname "Captain Crunch" from this whistle.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Toll-free_telephone_number#United_States" title="Toll-free telephone number">"toll-free" 800 service</a> was launched in 1967 and gave the hackers easy numbers to call. The user would generally choose a number in the target area and then use it as above. Even if billing information were generated, it would be to a 1-800 number and thus free of charge. As before, the remote system would notice a call going to the ultimate non-free number, but could not match the other end.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Technology">Technology</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_box&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Technology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>It was technically possible to generate the tones with the technology available at the time the system was first deployed. A <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a> or <a href="/wiki/Electronic_organ" class="mw-redirect" title="Electronic organ">electronic organ</a> had keys that were close enough in frequency to work. With tuning, they could even be made dead on frequency. For dialing the phone number, the user would press two keys at a time. An experienced pianist might have found the key combinations awkward to play. But a blank <a href="/wiki/Player_piano" title="Player piano">player piano</a> <a href="/wiki/Piano_roll" title="Piano roll">roll</a> could have been punched to operate the required keys and dial a phone number. Another strategy would have been to purchase <a href="/wiki/Doorbell" title="Doorbell">doorbells</a>, remove the plungers, and mount them on a frame that could be set over the piano keyboard. Twelve DPDT pushbuttons, labelled KP, ST and the 10 digits, would operate pairs of plungers to play the phone company tones, after the E7 piano key had been pressed and released.
</p><p>At the time, there were consumer devices for recording on wire or blank <a href="/wiki/Phonograph_record" title="Phonograph record">phonograph records</a>, so the piano did not have to be near the phone. Consumer <a href="/wiki/Tape_recorder" title="Tape recorder">tape recorders</a> came later and made the recording process easier. Small, <a href="/wiki/Electric_battery" title="Electric battery">battery</a> powered, tape recorders allowed the tones to be played back almost anywhere.
</p><p>It was possible to construct an electronic blue box with 1940s <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">vacuum tube</a> technology, but the device would have been relatively large and power-hungry. Just as it did for radios, shrinking them from the size of toasters to the size of cigarette packages and allowing them to be powered by small batteries, transistor technology made a small, battery-powered, electronic blue box practical.
</p><p>AT&amp;T security captured its first blue box in about 1962, but it probably was not the first one built.
</p><p>A typical blue box had 13 pushbuttons. One button would be for the 2600<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz tone, pressed and released to disconnect the outgoing connection and then connect a digit receiver. There would be a KP button, to be pressed next, 10 buttons for telephone number digits, and the ST button to be pressed last. The blue box may have had 7 oscillators, 6 for the 2 out of 6 digit code and one for the 2600<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz tone, or 2 oscillators with switchable frequencies.
</p><p>The blue box was thought to be a sophisticated electronic device and sold on the black market for a typical $800–1,000 or as much as $3,500. Actually, designing and building one was within the capabilities of many electronics students and engineers with knowledge of the required tones, using published designs for electronic oscillators, amplifiers and switch matrixes, and assembled with readily available parts. Furthermore, it was possible to generate the required tones using consumer products or lab test equipment. The tones could be recorded on small, battery-powered, cassette recorders for playback anywhere. In the early 1980s, <a href="/wiki/Radio_Shack" class="mw-redirect" title="Radio Shack">Radio Shack</a> sold<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> pairs of <a href="/wiki/Intersil_ICL8038" title="Intersil ICL8038">Intersil ICL8038</a> voltage-controlled oscillator chips which were ideal for the purpose.  A clever hack was to use a TI-30 <a href="/wiki/Calculator#Pocket_calculators" title="Calculator">pocket calculator</a> as the chassis of the device, with the diodes for the switch matrix wired into the keypad.  A miniature audio jack connected through the recharge port for the calculator's optional rechargeable battery would then be used to connect the speaker to play the tones into the handset.
</p><p>To reduce call setup time, telephone numbers were transmitted from machine to machine in a "speed dial" format, about 1.5 seconds for a 10-digit number, including KP and ST. To catch the cheaters, AT&amp;T could have connected monitors to digit receivers that were not being used for operator dialed calls and logged calls dialed at manual speed. So, some hackers went to the extra trouble of building blue boxes that stored telephone numbers and played the tones with the same timing as the machines.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Subculture">Subculture</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_box&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Subculture"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The widespread ability to blue box, once limited to just a few isolated individuals exploring the telephone network, developed into a subculture.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Famous phone phreaks such as "Captain Crunch", Mark Bernay,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTERosenbaum1971&#95;12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERosenbaum1971-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 125">&#58;&#8202;125&#8202;</span></sup> and Al Bernay used blue boxes to explore the various "hidden codes" that could not be dialled by a standard telephone.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Some of the more famous pranksters were <a href="/wiki/Steve_Wozniak" title="Steve Wozniak">Steve Wozniak</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steve_Jobs" title="Steve Jobs">Steve Jobs</a>, founders of <a href="/wiki/Apple_Computer" class="mw-redirect" title="Apple Computer">Apple Computer</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-jobsWozAtlantic&#95;13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jobsWozAtlantic-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> On one occasion, Wozniak dialed <a href="/wiki/Vatican_City" title="Vatican City">Vatican City</a> and identified himself as <a href="/wiki/Henry_Kissinger" title="Henry Kissinger">Henry Kissinger</a> (imitating Kissinger's German accent) and asked to speak to the Pope (who was sleeping at the time).<sup id="cite&#95;ref-iwoz&#95;14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iwoz-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-jobsWozAtlantic&#95;13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jobsWozAtlantic-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Wozniak said in 1986:<sup id="cite&#95;ref-stix19860514&#95;15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stix19860514-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1244412712">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 32px}.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;margin-top:0}@media(min-width:500px){.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{padding-left:1.6em}}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>I called only to explore the phone company as a system, to learn the codes and tricks. I'd talk to the <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> operator, and convince her I was a New York operator. When I called my parents and my friends, I paid. After six months I quit—I'd done everything that I could.
</p><p>I was so pure. Now I realize others were not as pure, they were just trying to make money. But then I thought we were all pure.
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Jobs later told his biographer that if it had not been for Wozniak's blue boxes, "there wouldn't have been an Apple."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="In_the_media">In the media</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_box&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In the media"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Blue boxing hit the mainstream media when an article by <a href="/wiki/Ron_Rosenbaum" title="Ron Rosenbaum">Ron Rosenbaum</a> titled <i>Secrets of the Little Blue Box</i> was published in the October 1971 issue of <i><a href="/wiki/Esquire_(magazine)" title="Esquire (magazine)">Esquire magazine</a></i>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTERosenbaum1971&#95;12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERosenbaum1971-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Suddenly, many more people wanted to get into the <a href="/wiki/Phone_phreaking" class="mw-redirect" title="Phone phreaking">phone phreaking</a> culture spawned by the blue box, and it furthered the fame of Captain Crunch.
</p><p>In June 1972, <a href="/wiki/Ramparts_(magazine)" title="Ramparts (magazine)"><i>Ramparts</i> magazine</a> printed the wiring schematics necessary to create a mute box (a variant of the blue box).<sup id="cite&#95;ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> All sold issues were recalled or seized from newsstands by police and officials of <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Bell" title="Pacific Bell">Pacific Bell</a>, causing financial loss to the magazine.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The magazine ceased operations for good in 1975.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The June 1975 issue of <a href="/wiki/73_(magazine)" title="73 (magazine)"><i>73</i> magazine</a> carried an article describing the rudiments of the long-distance signaling network, and how to construct and operate red and blue boxes.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This article included a blue box schematic using the Intersil 8038 voltage controlled oscillator chip.  Around the same time, do-it-yourself blue box <a href="/wiki/Electronic_kit" title="Electronic kit">kits</a> became available.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In November 1988, the <a href="/wiki/CCITT" class="mw-redirect" title="CCITT">CCITT</a> (now known as <a href="/wiki/ITU-T" title="ITU-T">ITU-T</a>) published recommendation Q.140 for the <a href="/wiki/Signaling_System_No._5" title="Signaling System No. 5">Signaling System No. 5</a>, which caused a resurgence of blue boxing by a new generation of users.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In the early 1990s, blue boxing became popular with the international <a href="/wiki/Warez_scene" title="Warez scene">warez scene</a>, especially in Europe. Software was made to enable blue boxing using a computer to generate and play the signaling tones. For the PC there were <a href="/wiki/BlueBEEP" title="BlueBEEP">BlueBEEP</a>, TLO, and others, and blue boxes were available for other platforms such as <a href="/wiki/Amiga" title="Amiga">Amiga</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Operation">Operation</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_box&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Operation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Automating_dialing">Automating dialing</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_box&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Automating dialing"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Local <a href="/wiki/Plain_old_telephone_service" title="Plain old telephone service">plain old telephone service</a> works by watching the voltage on the telephone lines between the telephone company's <a href="/wiki/Telephone_exchange" title="Telephone exchange">exchange office</a> and the customer's telephone. When the phone is on-hook ("hung up") the approximately 48<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">volt</a> electricity from the exchange flows to the phone and is looped back without passing through the handset. When the user picks up the handset, the current has to flow through the speaker and microphone in it, causing the voltage to drop to under 10<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>V. This sudden drop in voltage signals the user has picked up the phone.
</p><p>Originally, all calls were routed manually by an operator who would look for small <a href="/wiki/Light_bulb" class="mw-redirect" title="Light bulb">light bulbs</a> that would illuminate when a subscriber picked up the phone to make a call. The operator would connect a handset to the line, ask the user who they were calling, and then connect a cable between two <a href="/wiki/Phone_connector_(audio)" title="Phone connector (audio)">phone jacks</a> to complete the call. If the user was placing a long-distance call, the local operator would first talk to an operator at the remote exchange using one of the trunk lines between the two locations. When the local operator heard the remote customer come on the line, they would connect their local customer to the same trunk line to complete the call.
</p><p>The calling process began to be automated from the earliest days of the telephone system. Increasingly sophisticated <a href="/wiki/Electromechanical" class="mw-redirect" title="Electromechanical">electromechanical</a> systems would use the changes in voltage to start the connection process. The <a href="/wiki/Rotary_dial" title="Rotary dial">rotary dial</a> was introduced around 1904 to operate these switches; the dial repeatedly rapidly connects and disconnects the line, a process known as <a href="/wiki/Pulse_dialing" title="Pulse dialing">pulse dialing</a>. In common systems, these periodic changes in voltage caused a <a href="/wiki/Stepper_motor" title="Stepper motor">stepper motor</a> to rotate one position for each pulse of a digit, with longer pauses to switch from one rotary switch to another. When enough digits had been decoded, typically seven in North America, connections between the rotors would select a single line, the customer being dialed.
</p><p>The idea of using changing voltages to complete the call worked well for the local exchange where the distance between the customer and exchange office might be on the order of a few kilometers. Over longer distances, the <a href="/wiki/Capacitance" title="Capacitance">capacitance</a> of the lines filter out any rapid changes in voltage and dialing pulses do not reach the remote office in clean form, so that long-distance calls still required operator intervention. As telephone use grew, long-distance calling in particular, telephone companies were increasingly interested in automating this type of connection.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Long-distance_direct_dialing">Long-distance direct dialing</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_box&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Long-distance direct dialing"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>To address this need, the Bell System adopted a second system on the circuits that connected the exchanges. When the user dialed a long-distance number, indicated in North America by dialing a "1" at the beginning of the number, the call was switched to a separate system known as a "<a href="/wiki/Class-4_telephone_switch" title="Class-4 telephone switch">tandem</a>". The tandem would then buffer the remaining digits and decode the number to see which remote exchange was being dialed, generally using the <a href="/wiki/Area_code" class="mw-redirect" title="Area code">area code</a> for this purpose. They would then look for a free trunk line between the two exchanges; if none were available the tandem would play the "fast busy" reorder signal to tell the user to try again later.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTERosenbaum1971&#95;12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERosenbaum1971-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The basic protocol for finding a free line worked by playing a 2600<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz tone into the line whenever it was not being used. The tandems at both ends of a given trunk line did this. When the tandem determined which remote exchange was being called it scanned the trunk lines between the two exchanges looking for the tone. When it heard the tone on one of the lines, it knew that line was free to use. They would then select that line and drop the 2600<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz tone from their end. The remote tandem would hear the tone stop, drop their tone, and then play a <i>supervision flash</i>, making a "ka-cheep" sound, to indicate they had noticed the signal. The line was now free on both ends to connect a call.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTERosenbaum1971&#95;12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERosenbaum1971-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Pulse dialing still had the problem that sending the dialed number to the remote exchange would not work due to the capacitance of the network. The tandems solved this by buffering the phone number and then converting each digit into a series of two tones, the <a href="/wiki/Multi-frequency_signaling" title="Multi-frequency signaling">multi-frequency signaling</a> system, or "MF". Once the local tandem had found a free line and connected to it, it then relayed the rest of the phone number over the line using the tone dialing method. The remote tandem then decoded the tones and turned them back into pulses on the local exchange. To indicate the start and end of a series of MF digits, special MF tones, KP and ST, were used.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTERosenbaum1971&#95;12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERosenbaum1971-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>When the call was finished and one of the parties hung up the phone, their exchange would notice the change in voltage and begin playing the 2600<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz tone into the trunk line. The other end of the connection would respond to the tone by causing their local call to hang up as well, and then began playing the tone into their end as before, to mark the line as free at both ends.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTERosenbaum1971&#95;12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERosenbaum1971-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Blue_boxing">Blue boxing</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_box&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Blue boxing"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The blue box consisted of several of <a href="/wiki/Audio_oscillators" class="mw-redirect" title="Audio oscillators">audio oscillators</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Telephone_keypad" title="Telephone keypad">telephone keypad</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Audio_amplifier" class="mw-redirect" title="Audio amplifier">audio amplifier</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Loudspeaker" title="Loudspeaker">speaker</a>. To operate a blue box, the user placed a <a href="/wiki/Long-distance_telephone_call" class="mw-redirect" title="Long-distance telephone call">long-distance telephone call</a>, often to a number that was in the target area. Usually, this initial call would be to a <a href="/wiki/Toll-free_telephone_number" title="Toll-free telephone number">1-800 number</a> or some other non-supervising telephone number such as <a href="/wiki/Directory_assistance" title="Directory assistance">directory assistance</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTERosenbaum1971&#95;12-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERosenbaum1971-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Using a toll-free number ensured that the phone being used for access would not be billed.
</p><p>When the call began to ring, the caller would hold the blue box speaker over the microphone in the handset and use the box to send the 2600<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz tone (or 2600+2400<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz on many international trunks followed by a 2400<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz tone). The called office interpreted this tone as the caller hanging up before the call completed, disconnected the call, and began playing 2600<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz to mark the line free. However, this did not disconnect the call at the caller's end, but instead would leave the caller on a live line that was connected via a long-distance trunk line to a target exchange.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTERosenbaum1971&#95;12-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERosenbaum1971-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The caller would then stop playing the tone, which the exchange would interpret to mean the exchange's tandem was attempting to place another call. It responded by dropping its tone and then playing the flash to indicate it was ready to accept routing tones. Once the called end sent the supervision flash, the caller used the blue box to send a "Key Pulse" or "KP", the tone that starts a routing digit sequence, followed by either a telephone number or one of the numerous special codes that were used internally by the telephone company, then finished with a "Start" tone, "ST".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTERosenbaum1971&#95;12-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERosenbaum1971-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> At this point, the called end of the connection would route the call the way it was told, while the caller's local exchange would act as if the call was still ringing at the original number.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Countermeasures">Countermeasures</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_box&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Countermeasures"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Blue boxing remained rare until the early 1970s when the required systems began to drop in cost and the concept began to be more widely known. At the time, phreakers felt there was nothing <a href="/wiki/Bell_Telephone_Company" title="Bell Telephone Company">Bell Telephone</a> could do to stop blue boxing because it would require Bell to upgrade all their hardware.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FOOTNOTERosenbaum1971&#95;12-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERosenbaum1971-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>For the immediate term, Bell responded with a number of blue box detection and law enforcement countermeasures. Armed with records of all long-distance calls made, kept by both mechanical switching systems and newer <a href="/wiki/Electronic_switching_system" title="Electronic switching system">electronic switching systems</a>, including calls to <a href="/wiki/Toll-free_telephone_number" title="Toll-free telephone number">toll-free telephone numbers</a> which did not appear on customer bills, telephone security employees began examining those records looking for suspicious patterns of activity. For instance, at the time, calls to long-distance information, while answered, deliberately did not return the electrical "off hook" signal indicating that they had been answered. When an information call was diverted to another number that answered, the billing equipment would log that event. Billing computers processed the logs and generated lists of calls to information that had been answered with an off-hook tone. In the early days, the lists were probably intended to detect equipment malfunctions, but the follow-up investigation did lead to blue box users. After the toll free "800" service was inaugurated, the billing computers were also programmed to generate lists of lengthy calls to toll free numbers. While many of these calls were legitimate, telephone security employees would examine the lists and follow up irregularities.
</p><p>In this case, filters could be installed on those lines to block the blue box. Bell also would <a href="/wiki/Wiretap" class="mw-redirect" title="Wiretap">wiretap</a> the affected lines. In one 1975 case, the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Bell" title="Pacific Bell">Pacific Telephone Company</a> targeted one defendant's line with the following equipment:
</p>
<ul><li>A CMC 2600, a device which registers on a counter the number of times a 2600<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz tone is detected on the line;</li>
<li>A tape recorder, activated automatically by the CMC 2600 to record two minutes of telephone audio after each burst of 2600<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz activity; and</li>
<li>A Hekemian 51A, which replicates the functions of the CMC 2600 and also produces a paper tape print-out of outgoing calls. Ordinary calls were recorded in black ink and destination numbers called via the blue box were recorded in red ink.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li></ul>
<p>These actions resulted in several highly publicized trials.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Decline">Decline</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_box&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Decline"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The ultimate solution to the blue box vulnerability was to do what the phreakers thought impossible and upgrade the entire network. This process occurred in stages, some of which were already well underway in the early 1970s.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/T-carrier" title="T-carrier">T1</a> system was developed beginning in 1957 and began to be deployed around 1962. It digitized the voice signals so that they could be more efficiently carried in high-density connections between exchanges, carrying 24 lines on a single 4-wire connection. Depending on the network layout, the user might no longer be connected directly to a tandem, but instead to a local office that forwarded the signal over a T1 to a more distant exchange that did have the tandem. Simply due to the way the system worked, the supervisory signals had to be filtered out in order for the digitization of the analog signal to work. Recall that the 2600<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz tone was not dropped from the trunk until the line was connected all the way and would be mixed with other tones like the ringing or busy signal; when used over a T1 this tone mixed with other signals and caused a problem known as "quantization noise" that distorted the sound. These tones were thus filtered down on either side of the T1 connection. Thus it was difficult to blue box in such an environment, although successes are known.
</p><p>But blue boxing was eventually eliminated entirely for unrelated reasons. In the existing tandem-based network, completing a call required several stages communicating over the trunk line, even if the remote user never answered the call. As this process might take on the order of 10 to 15 seconds, the total wasted time across all of the trunk lines could be used to carry additional calls. To improve line usage, Bell began the development of the <a href="/wiki/Number_One_Electronic_Switching_System" title="Number One Electronic Switching System">Number One Electronic Switching System</a> (1ESS). This system performed all the calling and line supervision using a separate private line between the two offices. Using this system, when a long-distance call was placed the trunk line was not initially used. Instead, the local office sent a message containing the called number to the remote exchange using this separate channel. The remote office would then attempt to complete the call, and indicate this to the original office using the same private line. Only if the remote user answered would the systems attempt to find a free trunk line and connect, thereby reducing the use of the trunk lines to the absolute minimum.
</p><p>This change also meant the signaling system was available internally to the network on this separate line. There was no connection between the user lines and this signaling line, so there was no route by which the users could influence the dialing. The same rapid reduction in prices that made the blue box possible also led to the rapid reduction in cost of the ESS systems. First applied only to their busiest connections, by the 1980s, the latest <a href="/wiki/4ESS" class="mw-redirect" title="4ESS">4ESS</a> models and similar machines from other companies were deployed to almost all major exchanges, leaving only corners of the network still connected using tandems. Blue boxing worked if one connected to such an exchange, but could only be used end-to-end if the entire network between the two endpoints consisted only of tandems, which became increasingly rare and disappeared by the late 1980s.
</p><p>Analog long-distance transmission systems remained more cost effective for the long haul circuits until, at least, the 1970s. Even then, there was a huge installed base of analog circuits, and it made better economic sense to keep using them. It was not until competitor Sprint built its all digital, "quiet", network, where "you could actually hear a pin drop",<sup id="cite&#95;ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> that AT&amp;T took a multi-billion dollar write-off and upgraded its long-distance network to digital technology.
</p><p>The phreaking community that had emerged during the blue box era evolved into other endeavors and there currently exists a commercially published hacking magazine, titled <i><a href="/wiki/2600:_The_Hacker_Quarterly" title="2600: The Hacker Quarterly">2600</a></i>, a reference to the 2600<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz tone that was once central to so much of telephone hacking.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div style="clear:both;" class=""></div>
<p>Normally, the tone durations for passing numbers from machine to machine in a "speed dialing" format are on for 60<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>ms, with 60<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>ms of silence between digits. The 'KP' and 'KP2' tones are sent for 100<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>ms. KP2 (ST2 in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=R1_standard&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="R1 standard (page does not exist)">R1 standard</a>) was used for dialing internal Bell System telephone numbers. However, actual tone durations can vary slightly depending on location, switch type, and the machine status.
</p><p>For operators, technicians, and blue box phone phreakers, the tone durations would be set by how long the buttons were held down and, for silence, how long before manually pressing the next button.
</p><p>A blue box could have been constructed which would send the tones with machine-to-machine timing, with the number either stored in digital memory or a matrix of switches. In the switch matrix, there might be 10 rows for digits, each with 5 switches. Two switches would be moved to on, selecting the 2 tones. (KP and ST would be hard wired.) The 5 switches could be labelled 0, 1, 2, 4, and 7, with the user selecting pairs of switches adding to each digit, with special case 4 plus 7 for digit 0.
</p><p>Alternatively, the tones could be recorded on magnetic tape, which would be cut into pieces and spliced together, using a commercial splicer for accurate alignment. If the phreaker matched machine dialing and recorded at 7.5 ips (inches per second), the splices for tone and silence would be about 1/2-inch long, with KP 3/4-inch long. For more manageable splicing lengths, the phreaker could use a 15 ips tape recorder, which was less common, and double those lengths. For those without a 15 ips machine but having 2 tape recorders, the tones could be recorded an octave low at 7.5 ips, the pieces spliced together would be were double those lengths. The spliced tape would be re-recorded from a 7.5 ips machine to a 3.75 ips machine. The resulting recording could be played back at 7.5 ips. An interval of 2600<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz, to disconnect the trunk, followed by an interval of silence, to give enough time for a digit receiver to connect, would be added to precede KP.
</p><p>This set of MF tones was originally devised for <a href="/wiki/Bell_System" title="Bell System">Bell System</a> <a href="/wiki/Switchboard_operator" title="Switchboard operator">long-distance operators</a> placing calls manually, as well as machine to machine dialing, and predates the DTMF system used by subscribers. The leading 1 for customer dialed long-distance calls was not dialed. For operators, the line was muted during dialing, but, for customer telephones, it was only muted while a key was pressed. The DTMF frequencies were chosen to minimize the risk of customer talking while dialing, or background sounds, being registered as a digit or digits and resulting in a wrong number. Muting guarded against that happening during operator dialing, so the MF system did not have to be, and was not, so robust. The tones have a simple 200<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz spacing. For DTMF, harmonic relationships and intermodulation products were taken into account in the choice of tones.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Special_codes">Special codes</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_box&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Special codes"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Some of the special codes a person could get onto are in the chart below. "<a href="/wiki/Numbering_plan_area" class="mw-redirect" title="Numbering plan area">NPA</a>" is a telephone company term for 'area code'.
</p><p>Many of these appear to have been originally three-digit codes, dialed without the leading area code, and the format of destination numbers dialed to the international senders has changed at various points as the ability to call additional nations was added.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<li>NPA+101 – Plant Test – Toll Testing Board</li>
<li>NPA+102 – Plant Test – Milliwatt tone (1004<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz)</li>
<li>NPA+103 – Plant Test – Signaling test termination</li>
<li>NPA+104 – Plant Test – 2-way transmission and noise test</li>
<li>NPA+105 – Plant Test – Automatic Transmission Measuring System</li>
<li>NPA+106 – Plant Test – CCSA loop transmission test</li>
<li>NPA+107 – Plant Test – Par meter generator</li>

<li>914+151 – Overseas incoming (White Plains, NY)</li>

<p>Not all NPAs had all functions. As some NPAs contained multiple cities, an additional routing code was sometimes placed after the area code. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Area_code_519" class="mw-redirect" title="Area code 519">519</a>+044+121 may reach the <a href="/wiki/Windsor,_Ontario" title="Windsor, Ontario">Windsor</a> inward operator and 519+034+121 the <a href="/wiki/London,_Ontario" title="London, Ontario">London</a> inward operator 175&#160;km (109&#160;mi) distant, but in the same area code.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="In_other_countries">In other countries</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_box&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: In other countries"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Another signaling system widely used on international circuits (except those terminating in North America) was <a href="/wiki/CCITT" class="mw-redirect" title="CCITT">CCITT</a> Signaling System No. 4 (friendly named 'SS4').
</p><p>Technical definitions are specified in formerly CCITT (now <a href="/wiki/ITU-T" title="ITU-T">ITU-T</a>) Recommendations Q.120 to Q.139.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>This was also an in-band system but, instead of using multifrequency signals for digits, it used four 35 ms pulses of tone, separated by 35 ms of silence, to represent digits in four-bit binary code, with 2400<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz as a "0" and 2040<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz as a "1". The supervisory signals used the same two frequencies, but each supervisory signal started with both tones together (for 150 ms) followed, without a gap, by a long (350 ms) or short (100 ms) period of a single tone of 2400<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz or 2040<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz. Phreaks in Europe built System 4 blue boxes that generated these signals. Because System 4 was used only on international circuits, the use of these blue boxes was more specialized.
</p><p>Typically, a phreak would gain access to international dialing at low or zero cost by some other means, make a dialed call to a country that was available via direct dialing, and then use the System 4 blue box to clear down the international connection and make a call to a destination that was available only via operator service. Thus, the System 4 blue box was used primarily as a way of setting up calls to hard-to-reach operator-only destinations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>A typical System 4 blue box had a keypad (for sending four-bit digit signals) plus four buttons for the four supervisory signals (clear-forward, seize-terminal, seize-transit, and transfer-to-operator). After some experimentation, nimble-fingered phreaks found that all they needed was two buttons, one for each frequency. With practice, it was possible to manually generate all the signals with sufficient timing precision, including the digit signals. This made it possible to make the blue box quite small.
</p><p>A refinement added to some System 4 blue boxes was an anti-acknowledgment-echo guard tone. Because the connection between the telephone and the telephone network is two-wire, but the signaling on the international circuit operates on a four-wire basis (totally separate send and receive paths), signal-acknowledgment tones (single pulses of one of the two frequencies from the far end of the circuit after receipt of each digit) tended to be reflected at the four-wire/two-wire conversion point. Although these reflected signals were relatively faint, they were sometimes loud enough for the digit-receiving circuits at the far end to treat them as the first bit of the next digit, messing up the phreak's transmitted digits.
</p><p>What the improved blue box did was to continuously transmit a tone of some other frequency (e.g., 600<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hz) as a guard tone whenever it was not sending a System 4 signal. This guard tone drowned out the echoed acknowledgment signals so that only the blue box-transmitted digits were heard by the digit-receiving circuits at the far end.
</p>
