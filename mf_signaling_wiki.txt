					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Signaling method for telephony</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Telephony" title="Telephony">telephony</a>, <b>multi-frequency signaling</b> (<b>MF</b>) is a type of <a href="/wiki/Signaling_(telecommunications)" title="Signaling (telecommunications)">signaling</a> that was introduced by the <a href="/wiki/Bell_System" title="Bell System">Bell System</a> after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. It uses a combination of audible tones for address (<a href="/wiki/Telephone_number" title="Telephone number">telephone number</a>) transport and supervision signaling on <a href="/wiki/Trunking" title="Trunking">trunk lines</a> between central offices. The signaling is sent <i><a href="/wiki/In-band_signaling" title="In-band signaling">in-band</a></i> over the same channel as the bearer channel used for voice traffic.
</p><p>Multi-frequency signaling defines electronic signals that consist of a combination of two audible frequencies, usually selected from a set of six frequencies. Over several decades, various types of MF signaling were developed, including national and international varieties. The <a href="/wiki/CCITT" class="mw-redirect" title="CCITT">CCITT</a> standardization process specified the American Bell System version as <i>Regional Standard No. 1</i>, or <b>Signalling System R1</b>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and a corresponding European standard as <a href="/wiki/Signalling_System_R2" title="Signalling System R2">Signalling System R2</a>. Both were largely replaced by digital systems, such as <a href="/wiki/Signalling_System_7" class="mw-redirect" title="Signalling System 7">Signalling System 7</a>, which operate out-of-band on a separate data network.
</p><p>Because of the in-band transmission characteristic of MF signaling, the systems proved vulnerable to misuse and fraud by <a href="/wiki/Phone_phreaking" class="mw-redirect" title="Phone phreaking">phone phreaking</a> with devices such as a <a href="/wiki/Blue_box" title="Blue box">blue box</a>.
</p><p>Multifrequency signaling is a technological precursor of <a href="/wiki/Dual-tone_multi-frequency_signaling" class="mw-redirect" title="Dual-tone multi-frequency signaling">dual-tone multi-frequency signaling</a> (DTMF, <i>Touch-Tone</i>), which uses the same fundamental principle, but was used primarily for signaling address information and control signals from a user's telephone to the wire-center's Class-5 switch. DTMF uses a total of eight frequencies.
</p>

<p>Digits are represented by two simultaneous tones selected from a set of five (MF 2/5), six (MF 2/6), or eight (MF 2/8) frequencies. The frequency combinations are played, one at a time for each digit, to the remote <a href="/wiki/Multi-frequency_receiver" title="Multi-frequency receiver">multi-frequency receiver</a> in a distant <a href="/wiki/Telephone_exchange" title="Telephone exchange">telephone exchange</a>. MF is used for signaling in <a href="/wiki/Trunking" title="Trunking">trunking</a> applications.
</p><p>Using MF signaling, the originating telephone switch sends a start signal to seize the line, taking the circuit off-hook. The terminating office acknowledges the seizure with a ready state by responding with a wink start signal, which is a momentary off-hook condition. The originating office then sends address information to the terminating switch. In R1 MF signaling this address information normally is a KP tone, the numeric digits of the destination number, and an ST tone to indicate the end of the address. Other information may also be added, such as the caller's number, using KP2 as a delimiter.
</p><p>MF is a type of <a href="/wiki/In-band_signaling" title="In-band signaling">in-band signaling</a>. Depending on the type and configuration of switching equipment, it may or may not be audible to the telephone user, but the technology was vulnerable to abuse with a method called <a href="/wiki/Phreaking" title="Phreaking">phreaking</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Blue_box" title="Blue box">blue box</a> which generates the tones required to control remote telephone switches.
</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bell_System" title="Bell System">Bell System</a> published the following standards for MF tone timing:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<ul><li>KP (110 ms)</li>
<li>Digits 0-9, ST: 55 ms</li></ul>
<p>The interval between digits is the same as the digit duration (55 ms)
</p><p>These standards are, for the most part, still in place where MF signaling is in use in legacy exchanges. MF signaling is still used in North America for inter-office signaling, although it is increasingly rare.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Demise">Demise</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multi-frequency_signaling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Demise"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In-band signaling fell into disfavor in the <a href="/wiki/Public_switched_telephone_network" title="Public switched telephone network">public switched telephone network</a> (PSTN) as electronic switching systems displaced electro-mechanical switching systems, but legacy offices may still exist in some countries that are still using some electromechanical and other legacy switching equipment.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Out-of-band <a href="/wiki/Common_Channel_Signaling" class="mw-redirect" title="Common Channel Signaling">Common Channel Signaling</a> (CCS) became nearly universal by the end of the 20th century in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Benefits include higher connection establishment rate and better fraud security.
</p><p>Most 911 <a href="/wiki/Public_Safety_Answering_Point" class="mw-redirect" title="Public Safety Answering Point">Public Safety Answering Points</a> (PSAPs) use the MF format to identify the calling party to the PSAP when processing calls from <a href="/wiki/Mobile_Telephone_Switching_Office" title="Mobile Telephone Switching Office">Mobile Telephone Switching Offices</a> (MTSOs) and landline telephone exchanges.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This is based on an earlier system which used MF to identify the calling party to a <a href="/wiki/Feature_group" title="Feature group">feature group</a> 'D' (101xxxx) alternate long-distance provider.
</p>
