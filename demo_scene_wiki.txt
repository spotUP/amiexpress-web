<p>The <b>demoscene</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span><span title="/ɛ/: &#39;e&#39; in &#39;dress&#39;">ɛ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/oʊ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;code&#39;">oʊ</span><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="/iː/: &#39;ee&#39; in &#39;fleece&#39;">iː</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is an international <a href="/wiki/Computer_art" title="Computer art">computer art</a> <a href="/wiki/Subculture" title="Subculture">subculture</a> focused on producing <b>demos</b>: self-contained, sometimes extremely small, computer programs that produce <a href="/wiki/Audiovisual" title="Audiovisual">audiovisual</a> presentations. The purpose of a demo is to show off <a href="/wiki/Computer_programming" title="Computer programming">programming</a>, visual art, and musical skills. Demos and other demoscene productions (graphics, music, videos, games) are shared, voted on and released online at festivals known as <a class="mw-selflink-fragment" href="#Parties">demoparties</a>.
</p><p>The scene started with the <a href="/wiki/Home_computer" title="Home computer">home computer</a> revolution of the early 1980s, and the subsequent advent of <a href="/wiki/Software_cracking" title="Software cracking">software cracking</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Crackers altered the code of <a href="/wiki/Computer_game" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer game">computer games</a> to remove copy protection, claiming credit by adding introduction screens of their own ("<a href="/wiki/Crack_intro" title="Crack intro">cracktros</a>"). They soon started competing for the best visual presentation of these additions.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-WiderScreen&#95;2014-04-15&#95;2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WiderScreen_2014-04-15-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Through the making of intros and stand-alone demos, a new community eventually evolved, independent of the gaming<sup id="cite&#95;ref-reunanen2010&#95;3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reunanen2010-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 29–30">&#58;&#8202;29–30&#8202;</span></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Warez_scene" title="Warez scene">software sharing</a> scenes.
</p><p>Demos are informally classified into several categories, mainly of size-restricted intros. The most typical competition categories for intros are the 64k intro and the 4K intro, where the size of the executable file is restricted to 65536 and 4096 bytes, respectively. In other competitions the choice of platform is restricted; only <a href="/wiki/8-bit" class="mw-redirect" title="8-bit">8-bit</a> computers like the Atari 800 or Commodore 64, or the 16-bit <a href="/wiki/Amiga" title="Amiga">Amiga</a> or <a href="/wiki/Atari_ST" title="Atari ST">Atari ST</a>. Such restrictions provide a challenge for coders, musicians, and graphics artists, to make a device do more than was intended in its original design.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demoscene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:FC-SR-logo.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/FC-SR-logo.jpg/250px-FC-SR-logo.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="156" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/FC-SR-logo.jpg/500px-FC-SR-logo.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="400" /></a><figcaption><a href="/wiki/Second_Reality" title="Second Reality">Second Reality</a> is a demo by <a href="/wiki/Future_Crew" title="Future Crew">Future Crew</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-top10hacks&#95;4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-top10hacks-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></figcaption></figure>
<p>The earliest computer programs that have some resemblance to demos and <a href="/wiki/Demo_effect" title="Demo effect">demo effects</a> can be found among the so-called <a href="/wiki/Display_hack" class="mw-redirect" title="Display hack">display hacks</a>. Display hacks predate the demoscene by several decades, with the <a href="/wiki/First_video_game" class="mw-redirect" title="First video game">earliest examples</a> dating back to the early 1950s.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-display&#95;hacks&#95;5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-display_hacks-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Demos in the demoscene sense began as <a href="/wiki/Software_cracking" title="Software cracking">software crackers</a>' "signatures", that is, crack screens and <a href="/wiki/Crack_intro" title="Crack intro">crack intros</a> attached to software whose <a href="/wiki/Copy_protection" title="Copy protection">copy protection</a> was removed. The first crack screens appeared on the <a href="/wiki/Apple_II" title="Apple II">Apple II</a> in the early 1980s, and they were often nothing but plain text screens crediting the cracker or their group. Gradually, these static screens evolved into increasingly impressive-looking introductions containing animated effects and music. Eventually, many cracker groups started to release intro-like programs separately, without being attached to unlicensed software.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-demo&#95;or&#95;die&#95;6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-demo_or_die-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> These programs were initially known by various names, such as <i>letters</i> or <i>messages</i>, but they later came to be known as <i>demos</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In 1980, <a href="/wiki/Atari,_Inc." title="Atari, Inc.">Atari, Inc.</a> began using a looping demo with visual effects and music to show the features of the <a href="/wiki/Atari_8-bit_computers" title="Atari 8-bit computers">Atari 400/800 computers</a> in stores.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> At the 1985 <a href="/wiki/Consumer_Electronics_Show" class="mw-redirect" title="Consumer Electronics Show">Consumer Electronics Show</a>, Atari showed a demoscene-style demo for its latest 8-bit computers that alternated between a 3D walking robot and a flying spaceship, each with its own music, and animating larger objects than typically seen on those systems; the two sections were separated by the Atari logo.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The program was released to the public. Also in 1985, a large, spinning, checkered ball—casting a translucent shadow—was the signature demo of what the hardware was capable of when Commodore's <a href="/wiki/Amiga" title="Amiga">Amiga</a> was announced.
</p><p>Simple demo-like music collections were put together on the C64 in 1985 by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Deenen" title="Charles Deenen">Charles Deenen</a>, inspired by crack intros, using music taken from games and adding some homemade color graphics.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In the following year, the movement now known as the demoscene was born. The Dutch groups 1001 Crew and <a href="/wiki/The_Judges_(demogroup)" title="The Judges (demogroup)">The Judges</a>, both Commodore 64-based, are often mentioned<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (May 2018)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> among the earliest demo groups. While competing with each other in 1986, they both produced pure demos with original graphics and music involving more than just casual work, and used extensive hardware trickery. At the same time demos from others, such as <a href="/wiki/Antony_Crowther" title="Antony Crowther">Antony Crowther</a>, had started circulating on <a href="/wiki/Compunet" title="Compunet">Compunet</a> in the United Kingdom.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Culture">Culture</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demoscene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Culture"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The demoscene is mainly a European phenomenon.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-reunanen2009&#95;9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reunanen2009-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It is a competition-oriented subculture, with groups and individual artists competing against each other in technical and artistic excellence. Those who achieve excellence are dubbed "elite", while those who do not follow the demoscene's implicit rules are called "lamers"; such rules emphasize creativity over "ripping" (or else using with permission) the works of others, having good contacts within the scene, and showing effort rather than asking for help.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-reunanen2009&#95;9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reunanen2009-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Both this competitiveness and the sense of cooperation among demosceners have led to comparisons with the earlier <a href="/wiki/Hacker_culture" title="Hacker culture">hacker culture</a> in academic computing.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-reunanen2009&#95;9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reunanen2009-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 159">&#58;&#8202;159&#8202;</span></sup> The demoscene is a closed subculture, which seeks and receives little mainstream public interest.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-reunanen2010&#95;3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reunanen2010-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 4">&#58;&#8202;4&#8202;</span></sup> As of 2010<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Demoscene&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, the size of the scene was estimated at some 10,000.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-hartmann&#95;11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hartmann-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In the early days, competition came in the form of setting records, like the number of "bobs" (<a href="/wiki/Blitter_object" title="Blitter object">blitter objects</a>) on the screen per frame, or the number of <a href="/wiki/Commodore_64_demos" title="Commodore 64 demos">DYCP</a> (Different Y Character Position) scrollers on a C64.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> These days, there are organized competitions, or compos, held at <a href="/wiki/Demoparty" class="mw-redirect" title="Demoparty">demoparties</a>, although there have been some online competitions. It has also been common for <a href="/wiki/Disk_magazine" title="Disk magazine">diskmags</a> to have voting-based charts which provide ranking lists for the best coders, graphicians, musicians, demos and other things.
</p><p>In 2020, Finland added its demoscene to its national <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> list of <a href="/wiki/Intangible_cultural_heritage" title="Intangible cultural heritage">intangible cultural heritage</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It is the first digital subculture to be put on an intangible cultural heritage list.
In 2021, Germany and Poland also added its demoscene to its national UNESCO list of intangible cultural heritage,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> followed by Netherlands in 2023<sup id="cite&#95;ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> with Sweden and France in 2025.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Groups">Groups</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demoscene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Groups"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Demo_PC_BlackMaiden_Interceptor.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Demo_PC_BlackMaiden_Interceptor.jpg/250px-Demo_PC_BlackMaiden_Interceptor.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="145" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Demo_PC_BlackMaiden_Interceptor.jpg/500px-Demo_PC_BlackMaiden_Interceptor.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="659" data-file-height="381" /></a><figcaption>Interceptor by Black Maiden</figcaption></figure>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1236090951">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Demogroup" title="Demogroup">Demogroup</a></div>
<p>Demosceners typically organize in small groups, centered around a coder (<a href="/wiki/Computer_programmer" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer programmer">programmer</a>), a musician, a graphician (graphics designer) and a swapper (who spreads their own and others' creations by mail).
</p><p>Groups always have names, and similarly the individual members pick a handle by which they will be addressed in the large community. While the practice of using handles rather than real names is a borrowing from the cracker/warez culture, where it serves to hide the identity of the cracker from law enforcement, in the demoscene (oriented toward legal activities) it mostly serves as a manner of self-expression. Group members tend to self-identify with the group, often extending their handle with their group's name, following the patterns "<i>Handle</i> of <i>Group</i>" or "<i>Handle</i>/<i>Group</i>".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-reunanen2010&#95;3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reunanen2010-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 31–32">&#58;&#8202;31–32&#8202;</span></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Parties">Parties</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demoscene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Parties"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Assembly2004-areena01.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Assembly2004-areena01.jpg/250px-Assembly2004-areena01.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="188" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Assembly2004-areena01.jpg/500px-Assembly2004-areena01.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="600" /></a><figcaption><a href="/wiki/Assembly_(demoparty)" title="Assembly (demoparty)">Assembly</a> 2004 – a combination of a demoparty and a <a href="/wiki/LAN_party" title="LAN party">LAN party</a></figcaption></figure>
<p>A demoparty is an event where demosceners<sup id="cite&#95;ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and other computer enthusiasts gather to take part in competitions, nicknamed <i>compos</i>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> where they present demos (short <a href="/wiki/Audio-visual" class="mw-redirect" title="Audio-visual">audio-visual</a> presentations of <a href="/wiki/Computer_art" title="Computer art">computer art</a>) and other works such as digital art and music.  A typical demoparty is a non-stop event spanning a weekend, providing the visitors a lot of time to socialize. The competing works, at least those in the most important competitions, are usually shown at night, using a <a href="/wiki/Video_projector" title="Video projector">video projector</a> and <a href="/wiki/Loudspeaker" title="Loudspeaker">loudspeakers</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-demographics&#95;20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-demographics-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
</p><p>The most important competition is usually the <i>demo compo</i>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Assembly_(demoparty)" title="Assembly (demoparty)">Assembly</a> is the biggest demoscene party.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/The_Gathering_(LAN_party)" title="The Gathering (LAN party)">The Gathering</a> became more of a players' party, the worlds largest computerparty.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Concept">Concept</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demoscene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Concept"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The visitors of a demoparty often bring their own computers to compete and show their works. To this end, most parties provide a large hall with tables, electricity and usually a <a href="/wiki/Local_area_network" title="Local area network">local area network</a> connected to the Internet. In this respect, many demoparties resemble <a href="/wiki/LAN_party" title="LAN party">LAN parties</a>, and many of the largest events also gather gamers and other computer enthusiasts in addition to demosceners. A major difference between a real demoparty and a LAN party is that demosceners typically spend more time socializing (often outside the actual party hall) than in front of their computers.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="64K_intro">64K intro</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demoscene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 64K intro"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>A <b>64K intro</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Demo_(computer_programming)" class="mw-redirect" title="Demo (computer programming)">demo</a> with an executable file size limit of 64 <a href="/wiki/Kibibyte" class="mw-redirect" title="Kibibyte">kibibytes</a>, or 65,536 <a href="/wiki/Byte" title="Byte">bytes</a>. This is a traditional limit inherited from the maximum size of a <a href="/wiki/COM_file" title="COM file">COM file</a>. Demos traditionally were limited by RAM size, or later by storage size. By the early 1990s, demo sizes grew, so categories were created for limited sizes that forced developers to not simply stream data from storage.
</p><p>To reduce the file size, 64K intros often use <a href="/wiki/Executable_compression" title="Executable compression">executable compression</a> and <a href="/wiki/Procedural_generation" title="Procedural generation">procedural generation</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Sound_synthesis" class="mw-redirect" title="Sound synthesis">sound synthesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesh_generation" title="Mesh generation">mesh generation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Procedural_textures" class="mw-redirect" title="Procedural textures">procedural textures</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Procedural_animation" title="Procedural animation">procedural animation</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>fr-08, a 64k PC demo by <a href="/wiki/Farbrausch" title="Farbrausch">Farbrausch</a> released at <a href="/wiki/The_Party_(demoparty)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Party (demoparty)">The Party</a> 2000 in <a href="/wiki/Aars" title="Aars">Aars</a> has since been claimed<sup id="cite&#95;ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>44<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> to mark a <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/watershed_moment" class="extiw" title="wikt:watershed moment">watershed moment</a> in the popularity of the category. Others include <a href="/wiki/Chaos_Theory_(demo)" title="Chaos Theory (demo)">Chaos Theory</a> by <a href="/wiki/Conspiracy_(demogroup)" class="mw-redirect" title="Conspiracy (demogroup)">Conspiracy</a> (2006), Gaia Machina by Approximate (2012),<sup id="cite&#95;ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>45<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> F — Felix's Workshop by Ctrl-Alt-Test (2012)<sup id="cite&#95;ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>46<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Fermi paradox by Mercury (2016),<sup id="cite&#95;ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>47<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-siggraph&#95;48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-siggraph-48"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>48<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and Darkness Lay Your Eyes Upon Me by Conspiracy (2016).<sup id="cite&#95;ref-siggraph&#95;48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-siggraph-48"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>48<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Awards">Awards</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demoscene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Awards"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Every year, awards in the demoscene celebrate the creativity, technical prowess, and artistic vision of demoscene groups and individuals:
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Scene.org_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="Scene.org Awards">Scene.org Awards</a> were an annual award presented by Scene.org from 2003 to 2012.</li>
<li>The Meteoriks are an annual award that happens every year since 2014. The award ceremony is held at <a href="/wiki/Revision_(demoparty)" title="Revision (demoparty)">Revision</a>.</li></ul>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Influence">Influence</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demoscene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Influence"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Although demos are a rather obscure form of art, even in traditionally active demoscene countries, the scene has influenced areas such as <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">computer games industry</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_media_art" title="New media art">new media art</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>49<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>50<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>51<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Many European game programmers, artists, and musicians have come from the demoscene, often cultivating the learned techniques, practices and philosophies in their work. For example, the Finnish company <a href="/wiki/Remedy_Entertainment" title="Remedy Entertainment">Remedy Entertainment</a>, known for the <a href="/wiki/Max_Payne" title="Max Payne">Max Payne</a> series of games, was founded by the PC group <a href="/wiki/Future_Crew" title="Future Crew">Future Crew</a>, and most of its employees are former or active Finnish demosceners.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>52<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>53<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
Sometimes demos even provide direct influence even to game developers that have no demoscene affiliation: for instance, <a href="/wiki/Will_Wright_(game_designer)" title="Will Wright (game designer)">Will Wright</a> names demoscene as a major influence on the <a href="/wiki/Maxis" title="Maxis">Maxis</a> game <a href="/wiki/Spore_(computer_game)" class="mw-redirect" title="Spore (computer game)">Spore</a>, which is largely based on <a href="/wiki/Procedural_generation" title="Procedural generation">procedural content generation</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>54<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Similarly, at <a href="/wiki/QuakeCon" title="QuakeCon">QuakeCon</a> in 2011, <a href="/wiki/John_Carmack" title="John Carmack">John Carmack</a> noted that he "thinks highly" of people who do 64k intros, as an example of artificial limitations encouraging creative programming.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>55<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Holkins" title="Jerry Holkins">Jerry Holkins</a> from <a href="/wiki/Penny_Arcade" title="Penny Arcade">Penny Arcade</a> claimed to have an "abiding love" for the demoscene, and noted that it is "stuff worth knowing".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>56<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Certain forms of computer art have a strong affiliation with the demoscene. <a href="/wiki/Tracker_(music_software)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tracker (music software)">Tracker music</a>, for example, originated in the Amiga game industry but was soon heavily dominated by demoscene musicians; producer <a href="/wiki/Adam_Fielding_(musician)" title="Adam Fielding (musician)">Adam Fielding</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>57<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> claims to have tracker/demoscene roots. Currently, there is a major tracking scene separate from the actual demoscene. A form of static computer graphics where demosceners have traditionally excelled is <a href="/wiki/Pixel_art" title="Pixel art">pixel art</a>; see <i><a href="/wiki/Artscene" class="mw-redirect" title="Artscene">artscene</a></i> for more information on the related subculture.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Origins of <a href="/wiki/Creative_coding" title="Creative coding">creative coding</a> tools like <a href="/wiki/Shadertoy" title="Shadertoy">Shadertoy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Three.js" title="Three.js">Three.js</a> can be directly traced back to the scene.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>58<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Over the years, desktop computer hardware capabilities have improved by orders of magnitude, and so for most programmers, tight hardware restrictions are no longer a common issue. Nevertheless, demosceners continue to study and experiment with creating impressive effects on limited hardware. Since <a href="/wiki/Handheld_console" class="mw-redirect" title="Handheld console">handheld consoles</a> and cellular phones have comparable processing power or capabilities to the desktop platforms of old (such as low resolution screens which require pixel art, or very limited storage and memory for music replay), many demosceners have been able to apply their niche skills to develop games for these platforms, and earn a living doing so.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> One particular example is <i><a href="/wiki/Angry_Birds" title="Angry Birds">Angry Birds</a></i>, whose lead designer Jaakko Iisalo was an active and well-known demoscener in the 1990s.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>59<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Unity_Technologies" title="Unity Technologies">Unity Technologies</a> is another notable example; its technical leads on iPhone, Android and Nintendo Switch platforms Renaldas Zioma and Erik Hemming<sup id="cite&#95;ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>60<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>61<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> are authors of <i>Suicide Barbie</i><sup id="cite&#95;ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>62<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> demo for the Playstation Portable console, which was released in 2007.
</p><p>Some attempts have been made to increase the familiarity of demos as an art form. For example, there have been demo shows, demo galleries and demoscene-related books, sometimes even TV programs introducing the subculture and its works.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>63<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (September 2014)">original research?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The museum IT-ceum in Linköping, Sweden, has an exhibition about the demoscene.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>64<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Video_game_industry">Video game industry</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demoscene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Video game industry"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>4players.de reported that "numerous" demo and intro programmers, artists, and musicians were employed in the games industry by 2007. Video game companies with demoscene members on staff included <a href="/wiki/Digital_Illusions" class="mw-redirect" title="Digital Illusions">Digital Illusions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Starbreeze" class="mw-redirect" title="Starbreeze">Starbreeze</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ascaron" title="Ascaron">Ascaron</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-4sceners&#95;2007&#95;p1&#95;65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4sceners_2007_p1-65"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>65<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 49Games, <a href="/wiki/Remedy_Entertainment" title="Remedy Entertainment">Remedy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Housemarque" title="Housemarque">Housemarque</a>, <a href="/wiki/IO_Interactive" title="IO Interactive">IO Interactive</a>, <a href="/wiki/Techland" title="Techland">Techland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lionhead_Studios" title="Lionhead Studios">Lionhead Studios</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-4sceners&#95;2007&#95;p2&#95;66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4sceners_2007_p2-66"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>66<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bugbear_Entertainment" title="Bugbear Entertainment">Bugbear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Digital_Reality" title="Digital Reality">Digital Reality</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla_Games" title="Guerrilla Games">Guerrilla Games</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Akella" title="Akella">Akella</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-4sceners&#95;2007&#95;p3&#95;67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4sceners_2007_p3-67"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>67<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Music_tracker" title="Music tracker">tracker music</a> which is part of demoscene culture could be found in many video games of the late 1980s to early 2000s, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Lemmings_(video_game)" title="Lemmings (video game)">Lemmings</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Jazz_Jackrabbit" title="Jazz Jackrabbit">Jazz Jackrabbit</a></i>,  <i><a href="/wiki/One_Must_Fall:_2097" title="One Must Fall: 2097">One Must Fall: 2097</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Crusader:_No_Remorse" title="Crusader: No Remorse">Crusader: No Remorse</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Unreal_(video_game_series)" title="Unreal (video game series)"><i>Unreal</i> series</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Deus_Ex_(video_game)" title="Deus Ex (video game)">Deus Ex</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bejeweled_(video_game)" title="Bejeweled (video game)">Bejeweled</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Uplink_(video_game)" title="Uplink (video game)">Uplink</a></i>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>68<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
