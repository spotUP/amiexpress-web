					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Audible telecommunication signal for control of telephone networks</div>
<p><b>2600 hertz</b> (<b>2600&#160;Hz</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">hertz</a> (cycles per second) that was used in <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication_signaling" class="mw-redirect" title="Telecommunication signaling">telecommunication signaling</a> in mid-20th century <a href="/wiki/Long-distance_calling" title="Long-distance calling">long-distance</a> telephone networks using <a href="/wiki/Carrier_system" title="Carrier system">carrier systems</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-weaver_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weaver-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="side-box-text plainlist"><div class="haudio">
<div class="listen-file-header"><a href="/wiki/File:2600_Hz.ogg" title="File:2600 Hz.ogg">2600 Hz</a></div>
<div><span typeof="mw:File"><span><audio id="mwe_player_0" controls="" preload="none" data-mw-tmh="" class="mw-file-element" width="232" style="width:232px;" data-durationhint="2" data-mwtitle="2600_Hz.ogg" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons"><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fe/2600_Hz.ogg" type="audio/ogg; codecs=&quot;vorbis&quot;" data-width="0" data-height="0" /><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/f/fe/2600_Hz.ogg/2600_Hz.ogg.mp3" type="audio/mpeg" data-transcodekey="mp3" data-width="0" data-height="0" /></audio></span></span></div>
<div class="description">A 2600&#160;Hz tone</div></div></div></div>
<div class="side-box-abovebelow"><hr /><i class="selfreference">Problems playing this file? See <a href="/wiki/Help:Media" title="Help:Media">media help</a>.</i></div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tone_signaling" class="mw-redirect" title="Tone signaling">Tone signaling</a> carrier systems operated in the standard telephony voice frequency range (<span style="white-space:nowrap">300<span style="margin-left:0.25em">Hz</span></span> to <span style="white-space:nowrap">3500<span style="margin-left:0.25em">Hz</span></span>). They replaced <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">direct current</a> (D.C.) signaling on toll <a href="/wiki/Trunk_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Trunk line">trunk lines</a> because they could be used with any type of toll facility over any length of transmission line that was suitable for voice transmissions. This included transmission through line repeaters and other facilities that would distort, block, or otherwise prohibit D.C. loop-disconnect signaling, such as <a href="/wiki/Rotary_dial" title="Rotary dial">rotary dial</a> pulses, and on-hook/off-hook signaling. Common frequencies for this purpose were <span style="white-space:nowrap">1600<span style="margin-left:0.25em">Hz</span></span>, <span style="white-space:nowrap">2000<span style="margin-left:0.25em">Hz</span></span>, <span style="white-space:nowrap">2400<span style="margin-left:0.25em">Hz</span></span>, <span style="white-space:nowrap">2600<span style="margin-left:0.25em">Hz</span></span>, and <span style="white-space:nowrap">3700<span style="margin-left:0.25em">Hz</span></span>, the latter being just outside the voice range.<sup id="cite_ref-weaver_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weaver-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> These signaling systems were continuous tone methods, so that the idle condition of a trunk line could easily be detected by the presence of the appropriate signaling frequency, in contrast to conditions of a quiet line which could be due to pauses in speech, or arise from line faults. The tones were typically transmitted in the same physical and logical channel, which characterizes these systems as <a href="/wiki/In-band_signaling" title="In-band signaling">in-band signaling</a> methods, which do not require additional bandwidth for control of the network and benefit from a single amplification facility for speech and signaling.<sup id="cite_ref-Breen_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Breen-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The most common <a href="/wiki/Single-frequency_signaling" title="Single-frequency signaling">single-frequency signaling</a> (SF) system in use in the United States from the 1950s to the 1970s used the frequency pairing of 2600 with 2400 hertz, while in other countries, some systems combined <span style="white-space:nowrap">2600<span style="margin-left:0.25em">Hz</span></span> with a variety of lower frequencies in a compelled mode.  Lower frequencies, such as <span style="white-space:nowrap">1600<span style="margin-left:0.25em">Hz</span></span>, were used by the 1940s in composite signaling systems, however, the higher signal energy in speech at <span style="white-space:nowrap">1600<span style="margin-left:0.25em">Hz</span></span> presented technical problems in protection against false operation.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The in-band signaling method was vulnerable to <i>talk-off</i> conditions when the voice of a telephone user accidentally or intentionally generates the same tone or sufficiently strong spectral content at the frequency of the signaling system, a condition also known as <i><a href="/wiki/Falsing" title="Falsing">falsing</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In this instant, the call would be disconnected prematurely, and the trunk placed in idle condition. The <a href="/wiki/Bell_System" title="Bell System">Bell System</a> in the United States used special <i>signal-to-guard</i> arrangements in the signaling receiver to detect this condition by comparison with the energy in the frequency spectrum outside the signaling tone.<sup id="cite_ref-Breen_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Breen-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The condition was also often mitigated by narrow-band <a href="/wiki/Notch_filter" class="mw-redirect" title="Notch filter">notch filters</a> during the seized line state.<sup id="cite_ref-zucker_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zucker-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The discovery of this phenomenon by technology-curious individuals in the 1960s, led to the abuse by <a href="/wiki/Phreaking" title="Phreaking">phreaking</a>, a subculture that exploited the technology to explore national and international telephone networks and place cost-free long-distance telephone calls.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Combating abuse, and improving communications, the telephone industry transitioned to <a href="/wiki/Out-of-band_signaling" class="mw-redirect" title="Out-of-band signaling">out-of-band signaling</a> systems, such as  <a href="/wiki/Signalling_System_7" class="mw-redirect" title="Signalling System 7">Signalling System 7</a> (SS7), by the 1980s. This separated the voice and signaling <a href="/wiki/Communication_channel" title="Communication channel">channels</a>, making it impossible to generate control signals in the voice bearer channel. The development of the <a href="/wiki/T-carrier" title="T-carrier">T-carrier</a> system in the 1960s helped obsoleting single-frequency (SF) signaling. SF signaling was replaced by advanced methods of common channel signaling, a technology in development since the 1920s.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1970s, <a href="/wiki/Multi-frequency_signaling" title="Multi-frequency signaling">multi-frequency signaling</a> systems came into use for <a href="/wiki/IDDD" class="mw-redirect" title="IDDD">international direct distance dialing</a> (IDDD) which used the frequency of <span style="white-space:nowrap">2600<span style="margin-left:0.25em">Hz</span></span> in <a href="/wiki/Line_signaling" title="Line signaling">line signaling</a> in two-frequency mode with <span style="white-space:nowrap">2400<span style="margin-left:0.25em">Hz</span></span>, for line seizure during call setup, and for tear-down.
</p><p><span style="white-space:nowrap">2600<span style="margin-left:0.25em">Hz</span></span> signaling was a standard for many international signaling systems,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> such as the <i>Regional System R1</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The prominent application of the <span style="white-space:nowrap">2600<span style="margin-left:0.25em">Hz</span></span> frequency in telecommunications world-wide inspired the name of many <a href="/wiki/Hacker" title="Hacker">hacker</a> communities and publications, such as <i><a href="/wiki/2600:_The_Hacker_Quarterly" title="2600: The Hacker Quarterly">2600: The Hacker Quarterly</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and the late 20th-century <a href="/wiki/Counterculture" title="Counterculture">counterculture</a> <i>2600</i>.
